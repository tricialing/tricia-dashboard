How Listening Season sounds, and how to find it again when you've lost it
Written March 2026. For future-you in semester 6, exhausted and needing a reminder.

The Voice

Where it comes from

This voice was not designed. It was noticed. It's the way you talk when you've done the research and you want to share it, not perform it. When you're thinking out loud and inviting someone in rather than presenting a finished conclusion.

The quality to chase: the writer who can spend three paragraphs on something completely ordinary and make you feel like you've never actually looked at it before. A fig. A door frame. The way your jaw feels in the morning. That's the curiosity. Applied to the mundane, it finds something true. Miranda July has it. You have it too, just slowed down to the pace of a season, rooted in Delta light and the sound of birds earlier than yesterday.

You're not writing from the other side of the learning. You're writing from inside it. That's not a limitation. That's the whole point.

Write like someone who has done the research and wants to share it, not perform it. Direct but unhurried. Specific enough that the reader recognises themselves in the sentence.

The five qualities

Direct but unhurried
You say the real thing without circling it. No warming up, no hedging. But you're not rushing anywhere. The sentence lands and then there's space after it.
Specific and sensory
Not "I felt tired." The kind of tired that sits behind the eyes and makes everything slightly too loud. Not "spring energy." The jaw tightness, the vivid dreams, the sudden urge to rearrange your whole life.
Honest about the middle
You don't write as someone who has figured it out. You write as someone who is figuring it out. The jaw click, the semester you're in, the thing you looked up last Tuesday and are still turning over. This is not failure. It's the material.
First person, not navel-gazing
You write about yourself to help someone else recognise themselves. The move is always: here's what happened in me → here's what it might mean for you. The self-disclosure is in service of the reader, not the writer.
Short when that's honest
You don't pad. You don't perform thoroughness. If it's done in two sentences, it ends there. Long when the season calls for it. Short when that's more true.

How Chinese lives in the writing

驚蟄 appears and you don't immediately explain it. You let it sit. The meaning arrives through the sentence around it, not through a definition in brackets. It's atmosphere, not translation. It's the way a word can carry texture that the English version can't.

Sometimes there's a story in the character itself. The two radicals, what they mean separately, how they came together. That's not a footnote. That's content. Use it when it's interesting, not as a habit.

Not this
Too explained

驚蟄 (Jīngzhé), which means "Awakening of Insects," is the fifth solar term in the traditional Chinese calendar. It falls between March 5–19 and is associated with the Wood element and the Liver organ system.

This
Atmospheric, earns its space

驚蟄. Thunder wakes what has been sleeping. I felt it before I knew what to call it. The restlessness, the vivid dreams, the sudden need to begin everything at once. The Liver does this in spring. It wants to move.

Length and format

You write long first and cut back. That's the right instinct. Don't fight it. The first draft is the excavation. The edit is where the voice clarifies.

Instagram
Short. One idea. One image. Often ends on a question or an observation rather than a conclusion. No hashtag soup. A few, chosen, at the end if at all.
Newsletter
As long as it needs to be. This is where you think out loud properly. One solar term, one thread. Can meander. Should feel like a letter to a specific person, not a broadcast.
Zine
Layered. Short blocks with space between them. The design carries as much as the words. You're not filling space. You're placing things.
Just Noodling
The most naked. Weirder, more personal, less resolved. The fig wasp facts, the midnight thoughts, the things that don't fit anywhere else. The interior arc alongside the public one.

The rules, for when you're tired and can't remember

01 · Say the specific thing, not the general thing.
Not "honour your body." The jaw click in the morning. The craving for something sour. The specific detail is the portal.

02 · Don't explain what the reader can feel.
If you've described the sensation accurately, you don't need to tell them what it means. Trust them to arrive there.

03 · One idea per piece.
Especially on Instagram. The urge to say everything at once is real. Resist it. One door, opened fully, is more useful than five doors ajar.

04 · Write the draft. Then ask: what is this actually about?
The first draft often buries the real point in paragraph three. Find it. Put it first or last. Delete the rest.

05 · Don't perform wellness.
The moment the writing starts to sound like it has everything figured out, stop. Go back to what's actually true right now. The semester you're in. The thing you looked up but still don't fully understand. The question you haven't answered yet.

06 · If it sounds like a caption, it's not done yet.
Generic seasonal content is everywhere. Your job is to say the thing nobody else is saying because they haven't lived it.

What this voice is not

For the moments when comparison makes it tempting to sound like someone else.

Not guru
You don't have the answers. You have the questions and the research and the living experiment of your own body. That's enough.
Not soft-wellness
No "nourish yourself." No "hold space." No language that has been worn smooth by overuse. If a phrase could appear on a candle, don't use it.
Not clinical
TCM has enough textbooks. You're translating it into something a person can feel in their body without a degree. Precision without distance.
Not performatively raw
Honesty in service of the reader, not oversharing in service of relatability. The difference is intention. Ask: am I sharing this because it's useful, or because it makes me seem real?

When you've lost it, come back to this

She is where I was. The work I'm doing publicly is the map I wish I'd had.

That's the whole voice. Everything else is just technique.

Write to her. She's curious. She grew up with this culture somewhere in the background and never fully followed the thread. She's in a season of life where the body is asking more of her attention than it used to. She doesn't want to be fixed. She wants to understand the system. She is not looking for a wellness influencer with a morning routine. She is looking for someone who is honest about being in the middle of it.

You are that person. You don't have to perform anything. Just say what's true.
